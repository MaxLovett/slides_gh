Macrointerest

Why political interest

  • It is the foundation (Almond and Verba 1963; Peterson et al. 2022):
    • Widespread civic engagement
    • Governmental accountability
    • Institutional effectiveness of democracy
  • It is the way (Miller et al. 2023; Lane, Do, and Molina-Rogers 2022; Foa and Mounk 2016):
    • Information process and attitude development
    • Quality of democratic decisions
    • Political indifference → political distrust, corruption, etc.

Why macrointerest

Macrointerest: Aggregative political interest of a country (Peterson et al. 2022)

  • Common standard of democracy
  • (Literally), the will of the people
  • Myth of macrointerest
    • Stability in microinterest → stability in macrointerest (Prior 2010)
    • Coexistence of “individual-level stability and aggregate-level volatility” (Peterson et al. 2022, 202)

Why should Chinese politics care

  • Macrointerest → participation order (熊易寒 2008)
  • Macrointerest → governance efficiency (孟天广 and 宁晶 2018)
  • Macrointerest → perceptive state accountability (李莹 and 林功成 2015)
  • Tones of DON’T KNOW yet

Don’t knows

  • How does political interest affect Chinese public’s institutional confidence and political participation?—We barely know (李莹 and 林功成 2015; 夏智 and 李跃华 1991; 张富良 2004)
  • What factors affect Chinese people’s political interest—We barely know (Chen and Zhong 1999; Zhong and Hu 2013; Zhong and Kim 2005)
  • How does the macrointerest of China evolve?—We don’t know
  • Is Chinese macrointerest different from other democracies and other regimes?—We don’t know
  • How does macrointerest vary across institutional settings?—We don’t know ✓
  • How does macrointerest respond to economic development?—We don’t know ✓

Analysis of Macrointerst

Theoretical Inferences

What Explains the Variances in Macrointerest?

  1. Campaigns and elections → Decisions on important political positions → Macrointerest ↑ (Larsen 2022)
  2. Inclusive institutions → Every group’s opinion matters → Macrointerest ↑ (Kittilson and Schwindt-Bayer 2010)
  3. Bad time → Public demand for accountability → Macrointerest ↑ (Peterson et al. 2022)
  4. Good time → More resources/more shaped mindset → Macrointerest ↑ (Inglehart and Welzel 2005; Solt 2008; Stimson 2015)

Empirical Obstacle and Solution

Obstacle:

Rich data, different set, no complete panel, sparseness across countries

Solution:

  • Dynamic Comparative Public Opinion
    • A method to allow cross-national, over-time, multi-survey comparison
  • Global dataset
    • 128 countries, 40 years

Design:

  • Democratic Electoral Systems (DES): Institutional features
  • Gallagher index: disproportionality
  • Eco development : OECD.Stat, SWIID

Research Design

  • Test macrointerest in the socioeoconomically most “developed” societies: OECD members
  • Bayesian multilevel model with intercepts varying across countries (Shor et al. 2007)
  • ‘Within-between random effects’ specification (Bell and Jones 2015)
    • Within: Short-term effects
    • Between: Long-term, historical effects

Findings

Conclusion

  • Small but well-estimated increases in political interest in election years
  • Parliamentarism, federalism, and proportionality yield greater macrointerest
  • Good times that lead the public to take interest in politics.
  • Future steps
    • Developing areas
    • China
    • Mechanism analysis
